this app is a color study

nine circles share one color. same hex, same value. but each one appears to change because it sits inside a different palette.

that trick is called simultaneous contrast. a color is never only itself, it is shaped by the colors around it.

every hue lives somewhere on a circle from 0 to 360 degrees. red is near 0, yellow near 60, green near 120, cyan near 180, blue near 240, magenta near 300, then back to red again.

a palette philosophy is a rule for choosing colors from that circle.

monochromatic stays with one hue, changing only lightness and saturation.

analogous uses nearby hues, so the palette feels close and natural.

complementary pairs opposite hues for clear contrast.

split complementary uses two colors near the opposite hue, keeping contrast while feeling a little softer.

triadic spaces three hues evenly around the wheel.

diary + saves the last sampled color into your color diary. open diary from the menubar to review saved swatches, right-click any swatch to copy or delete it, clear all entries, or export the full diary grid as a jpg.

tetradic uses four positions, giving the palette more energy and variety.

hue chooses the starting point. strange decides how closely the grid follows the rule.

low strange keeps things orderly. high strange lets colors wander.

the grid is arranged in diagonal bands, so colors gather into small neighborhoods instead of scattering at random. choose a style, reroll the hue, sample a color, copy its hex, and watch the same center circle keep changing as the world around it changes.

file>import photo distills an image into Chroma's color energy. export unique palette hex values from file>export palette or use the Export Palette button.

techno mode is something else entirely. it does not follow the normal rules of saturation or lightness. it pits soft colors against harsh ones on purpose, so the image feels unstable, electric, almost wrong, but still deliberate.

Techno Mode Theory
X

techno mode starts with an underlying color rule, then deliberately introduces controlled deviation.

it still uses a base hue, a palette structure, and grouped color relationships. the composition is not random. nearby cells are organized so the image maintains coherence.

the difference is that harmony is no longer the main objective. the system is designed to increase perceptual tension.

the palette is divided into contrasting color behaviors. some colors are shifted toward lighter, lower-saturation pastel values. others are pushed toward brighter, higher-saturation neon values.

the result is structured conflict. colors remain part of a system, but their perceived behavior becomes less stable.

hue, saturation, and lightness are allowed to drift beyond the normal limits of the original palette rule. certain palette philosophies are too orderly to support this level of tension, so techno mode replaces them with a more volatile internal logic.

what remains is a color system with a clear internal logic and an unstable perceptual surface. it keeps enough structure for the image to hold together, while introducing enough deviation for the image to vibrate.

the colors are not random. they are being tested against their surroundings.
