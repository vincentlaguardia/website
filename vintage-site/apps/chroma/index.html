Theory
X

this is a color study

all nine circles are the same color, but each one appears to change because it sits inside a different palette.

that trick is called simultaneous contrast. a color is never only itself, it is shaped by the colors around it.

every hue lives somewhere on a circle from 0 to 360 degrees. red is near 0, yellow near 60, green near 120, cyan near 180, blue near 240, magenta near 300, then back to red again.

a palette philosophy is a rule for choosing colors from that circle.

monochromatic stays with one hue, changing only lightness and saturation.

analogous uses nearby hues, so the palette feels close and natural.

complementary pairs opposite hues for clear contrast.

split complementary uses two colors near the opposite hue, keeping contrast while feeling a little softer.

triadic spaces three hues evenly around the wheel.

diary + saves the last sampled color into your color diary. open diary from the menubar to review saved swatches, right-click any swatch to copy or delete it, clear all entries, or export the diary as a jpg.

tetradic uses four positions, giving the palette more energy and variety.

hue chooses the starting point. strange decides how closely the grid follows the rule.

low strange keeps things orderly. high strange lets colors wander.

the grid is arranged in diagonal bands, so colors gather into small neighborhoods instead of scattering at random. choose a style, reroll the hue, sample a color, copy its hex or rgb, and see how the same color changes from place to place.

file>import photo distills an image into Chroma's color energy. export unique palette hex values from file>export palette or use the Export Palette button.

techno mode is something else entirely. it does not follow the normal rules of saturation or lightness. it knows where pastels and neons should never meet, and puts them there anyway. not every hue combination it finds has a name. use it carefully.

Techno
X

techno mode is not a random color effect. it is a structured system for increasing visual conflict while preserving an underlying grid and palette logic.

the important point is that techno mode does not redesign the composition. the structure remains stable. what changes is the behavior of the colors inside that structure.

techno mode also narrows the available palette philosophies. the full system can use monochromatic, analogous, complementary, split-complementary, triadic, and tetradic relationships. techno mode keeps monochromatic, analogous, split-complementary, and tetradic, but blocks complementary and triadic.

that restriction matters because complementary and triadic palettes are already highly legible. complementary is cleanly binary, while triadic is evenly balanced around the hue wheel. techno mode avoids those more resolved systems and favors palette structures that can bend, drift, and become less predictable without fully collapsing.

techno mode is therefore not simply making the palette “more colorful.” it changes the internal behavior of the palette. the system introduces stronger separation between soft, atmospheric color and harsher, more synthetic color, then places those states inside the same organized field.

the hue behavior becomes much more aggressive as well. techno mode expands hue drift to a minimum of 80° and a maximum of 300°, depending on the strange slider. on a 360° hue wheel, that means the drift can cover from 22.2% to 83.3% of the full wheel.

that is a major shift. colors can move far away from their original palette relationships, sometimes far enough to feel like they belong to a different harmony system entirely.

what prevents this from becoming chaos is the spatial logic. even in techno mode, colors are still assigned through band/group logic. the image keeps its organization, but the relationships inside that organization become more tense.

the result is controlled instability. techno mode keeps the palette engine and compositional logic, but pushes hue, saturation, and lightness far enough that neighboring colors begin to feel incompatible.
