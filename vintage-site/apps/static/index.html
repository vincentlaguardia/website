Static Coefficient Model for Textile Blends

Dr. Vincent J. Pastrami
A Quantitative Framework for Estimating Electrostatic Behavior in Garment Fabrics
Static Coefficient Calculator · Technical Reference
Triboelectric position weighted by moisture-dependent charge retention
Abstract. This paper describes a computational model for estimating the electrostatic charge-building tendency of multi-fiber textile blends as a function of fiber composition and ambient relative humidity. The model combines a charge-generation term derived from triboelectric series displacement and a charge-retention term derived from fiber moisture regain, yielding a dimensionless Static Coefficient (SC) on a 0 to 100 scale. The approach provides practitioners with a rapid, parameter-transparent screening tool for predicting cling, shock, and dust-attraction behavior across garment compositions.

1. Introduction

Electrostatic charge accumulation in clothing is a persistent and practically significant phenomenon with consequences ranging from nuisance-level garment cling to safety-critical discharge in low-humidity industrial environments. The primary drivers of static build-up in textiles are well established: (1) the position of the constituent fibers on the triboelectric series, which governs the polarity and magnitude of contact charge transfer between dissimilar surfaces, and (2) the ability of the fiber to retain that charge long enough to produce a measurable effect, which is governed by electrical surface resistivity and hence by moisture content.

Existing laboratory methods (surface resistivity measurement per IEC 61340-5-1, charge decay testing, and body voltage measurement) provide authoritative characterizations but require specialized equipment and sample preparation. The model presented here is an illustrative, parameter-transparent approximation intended for blend-design screening and educational use. It should not replace laboratory measurement where safety or regulatory compliance is at stake.

2. Theoretical Background

2.1 The Triboelectric Series

When two dissimilar materials are brought into contact and then separated, charge transfers from the material higher on the triboelectric series to the one lower on the series. The series is not perfectly reproducible across all experimental conditions; surface finish, contact pressure, temperature, and contamination all shift positions, but the broad ordering is consistent. For textile fibers the relative ordering, from most positive to most negative, runs approximately: wool → nylon → silk → linen/rayon → cotton → acetate → acrylic/polyester.

The model encodes fiber triboelectric position on a 1 to 100 integer scale. Wool, the most positive common textile fiber, anchors the positive end; acrylic and polyester anchor the negative end. A neutral reference point near position 66 separates fibers that tend to accumulate positive charge from those that accumulate negative charge. The absolute deviation from this neutral point is the key quantity that determines charge-generation potential: fibers at the extremes generate large contact potentials; fibers near the neutral point generate small ones regardless of polarity.

2.2 Moisture Regain and Surface Resistivity

Surface electrical resistivity determines how rapidly accumulated charge dissipates. Hygroscopic fibers adsorb water from ambient air; adsorbed water dramatically reduces surface resistivity, providing a conductive pathway for charge leakage. The quantity standardly used to characterize fiber hygroscopicity is moisture regain (MR): the mass of water held by a fiber specimen at equilibrium, expressed as a percentage of the conditioned (dry) fiber mass. Standard conditioning conditions are 20 °C and 65% relative humidity (RH). Regain values range from roughly 0.4% for polyester to 16% for wool.

The link between moisture content and resistivity is well documented in published measurements. At 65% RH, cotton and wool exhibit surface resistivities on the order of 109 to 1010 Ω/sq, while polyester and acrylic remain near 1013 to 1014 Ω/sq under the same conditions. At 20% RH, resistivities for all fibers shift upward by two to four orders of magnitude, which explains the pronounced increase in static complaints during winter heating seasons. These figures are consistent with measurements reported by Holme, McIntyre, and Shen (1998) and with the charge-decay test results compiled by Hersh and Montgomery (1955).

The effect of ambient humidity on in-use moisture content is approximately proportional: doubling the ambient RH relative to the standard condition doubles the equilibrium moisture content at first approximation. This gives a simple humidity-scaling factor of RH / 65 applied to the standard regain value.

2.3 Charge Decay and Dissipation Kinetics

When a static charge is deposited on a textile surface, it dissipates through two mechanisms: conduction along fiber surfaces (dominant in high-regain fibers) and slow volume conduction through the fiber bulk (dominant in synthetics). The time constant for surface dissipation scales inversely with surface conductivity and therefore with moisture content. Henry (1953) measured charge half-lives ranging from under 0.1 s for conditioned cotton at 65% RH to several hundred seconds for polyester at 20% RH, directly confirming that regain is the governing variable for practical charge persistence.

The retention factor R in this model is a static approximation of steady-state charge level rather than a decay time constant. It captures the asymptotic charge level reached when generation and dissipation come into equilibrium during normal garment wear, which is the quantity most relevant to cling and shock complaints.

3. Model Description

3.1 Blend Properties

For a garment comprising n fiber types with mass fractions wi, the blend triboelectric value Tblend and the blend standard moisture regain MRblend are computed as weighted arithmetic means:

Tblend = Σ wi · Ti     MRblend = Σ wi · MRi

This linear blending rule is physically justified for conductance-dominated dissipation (moisture regain) and is a standard simplification for the triboelectric position of heterogeneous surfaces. It underestimates interaction effects at fiber interfaces but is adequate for first-order screening. Lowell and Rose-Innes (1980) show that for most practical surface contacts, charge transfer magnitude is approximately proportional to the difference in work function (or triboelectric position) between the contacting materials, supporting the linearity assumption for blend averages.

3.2 Generation Potential G

The generation potential is the normalized triboelectric displacement from the neutral reference point N = 66:

G = min(1, |Tblend − N| / S)

where S = 40 is a scaling span chosen so that the most extreme common fibers (wool at T = 88, polyester at T = 36) reach generation potentials near unity. The absolute value ensures that both positive and negative extreme fibers are treated equivalently; the model does not distinguish polarity because the practical consequence (charge magnitude) is polarity-independent for cling and shock effects. The minimum clamps G to 1.0 for hypothetical fibers beyond the calibration range.

3.3 Retention Factor R

The retention factor models charge dissipation via moisture. Effective in-use moisture content Meff is scaled from the standard regain by the ambient-to-standard RH ratio:

Meff = MRblend × (RH / 65)

The retention factor is then modeled as a Langmuir-type saturation function:

R = 1 / (1 + β · Meff)

where β = 0.5 is an empirical sensitivity constant. This functional form produces R = 1.0 at zero moisture (perfect retention, no dissipation) and a smooth, monotonically decreasing curve as moisture rises. The choice of a hyperbolic rather than exponential form keeps R always positive and bounded in [0, 1], matching the physical requirement that retention cannot be negative and cannot exceed unity.

3.4 Static Coefficient SC

The final Static Coefficient is the product of the two independent factors, scaled to a 0 to 100 index:

SC = 100 × G × R

This product form reflects the physical reality that both generation and retention must be non-trivial for observable static to occur: a fiber that generates charge strongly but dissipates it instantly (low R) produces no net effect, and a fiber with perfect retention but negligible generation potential likewise produces no effect. The five output bands (CALM, LOW, MODERATE, HIGH, VOLATILE) are calibrated against the single-fiber SC values of the reference fibers and practical experience of when cling and shock become noticeable.

6. Worked Example

Consider a 60% cotton / 40% polyester blend at 40% ambient RH:

Tblend = 0.60 × 68 + 0.40 × 36 = 40.8 + 14.4 = 55.2
MRblend = 0.60 × 8.5 + 0.40 × 0.4 = 5.1 + 0.16 = 5.26%
G = |55.2 − 66| / 40 = 10.8 / 40 = 0.270
Meff = 5.26 × (40 / 65) = 3.24%    R = 1 / (1 + 0.5 × 3.24) = 0.381
SC = 100 × 0.270 × 0.381 ≈ 10.3   →   CALM

The cotton majority pulls the blend toward the neutral zone (moderate generation) while the cotton moisture regain at 40% RH provides sufficient dissipation to keep the final score in the CALM band. Replacing cotton with wool (T = 88, MR = 16%) would push the blend to higher generation and higher retention simultaneously, substantially raising SC.

A second example: 100% polyester at 20% RH (winter, heated indoors):

G = |36 − 66| / 40 = 30 / 40 = 0.750
Meff = 0.4 × (20 / 65) = 0.123%    R = 1 / (1 + 0.5 × 0.123) = 0.942
SC = 100 × 0.750 × 0.942 ≈ 70.6   →   VOLATILE

The combination of high generation potential (polyester sits far below the neutral point) and near-zero moisture dissipation at low humidity drives the score deep into the VOLATILE range, consistent with the well-known static cling behavior of synthetic garments worn in dry winter environments.

7. Empirical Basis and Model Validation

The triboelectric positions assigned to each fiber are derived from consensus values across the published series literature, principally Morton and Hearle (2008), Turki et al. (2015), and Holme, McIntyre, and Shen (1998). These sources converge on the same broad ordering (nylon and wool at the positive extreme, polyester and acrylic at the negative extreme) with minor disagreement in the mid-range positions of silk, linen, and acetate, where surface contamination and fiber processing have outsized effects.

Moisture regain values are drawn from standardized textile test data at 20 °C and 65% RH as tabulated in Morton and Hearle (2008), Table 3.1, and cross-checked against ASTM D1909 standard reference values for commercial fiber grades. The β = 0.5 sensitivity constant in the retention formula was set so that the model reproduces the qualitative pattern observed by Hersh and Montgomery (1955): cotton and wool at 65% RH are effectively static-free, while nylon at 40% RH shows mild static and polyester at 20% RH shows severe static. No regression against measured charge voltages was performed; the model is calibrated to qualitative ordering, not absolute charge values.

Published body voltage measurements on human subjects wearing single-fiber garments at controlled humidity (Shaw, 1917; Henry, 1953) show peak voltages scaling with both fiber triboelectric position and ambient dryness, consistent with the product structure SC = 100 × G × R. Specifically, pure wool garments at 20% RH consistently generate body voltages in the range of several kilovolts, while 65% RH suppresses voltages below the perception threshold (approximately 2 kV for shock sensation and 500 V for cling forces on lightweight fabrics). The VOLATILE and CALM thresholds in this model are aligned to these empirical breakpoints.

8. Limitations and Scope

The model is an illustrative approximation and should be interpreted accordingly:

Linear blending. Weighted-average triboelectric position is a simplification. In practice, the surface layer of a yarn or woven structure (not the bulk) governs triboelectric behavior, so face fiber and yarn architecture can shift outcomes beyond what composition alone predicts.

Temperature. The model uses only RH as an environmental variable. Temperature affects both triboelectric series ordering and moisture adsorption kinetics and is not modeled here.

Fabric construction. Yarn twist, weave density, surface finish, and laundering history all affect charge generation and dissipation but are outside the scope of this fiber-level model.

Polarity. The model treats generation potential as polarity-independent. In garment systems where two garment layers interact, polarity matters for predicting whether layers attract or repel each other.

Fiber finishes and treatments. Antistatic finishes, silicone softeners, and soil-release coatings alter surface resistivity independently of fiber composition. A fabric treated with a durable antistatic agent can exhibit SC behavior two or three bands below the unfinished prediction. These treatments are not captured by this model.

Calibration. The scale constants (N = 66, S = 40, β = 0.5, band thresholds) are set to produce intuitive single-fiber rankings consistent with published qualitative assessments. They are not derived from a regression against a labeled dataset of measured charge values.
